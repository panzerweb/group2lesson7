Women and Public Life

The Grant of Suffrage to Filipino Women

Until the mid-1930s, only men in the Philippines had the right to vote. The 1935 Constitution initially restricted suffrage to male citizens aged 21 and above. However, it included a provision allowing women to vote if at least 300,000 qualified women approved it in a plebiscite.

This led to Commonwealth Act No. 34, which facilitated a plebiscite on April 30, 1937. Nearly 500,000 women voted in favor, granting Filipino women the right to vote and run for office. Since then, women have played an active role in Philippine politics, including the election of two female presidents: Corazon Aquino (1986-1992) and Gloria Macapagal-Arroyo (2001-2010).

The 1973 and 1987 Constitutions further expanded voting rights by lowering the voting age from 21 to 18 years old. Today, Filipino women hold key positions across the government, including in the Senate, House of Representatives, Judiciary, and national commissions.

This historic milestone remains one of the most significant achievements of the Philippine Commonwealth Government (1935-1946) in advancing women's rights and political participation.

First Woman President of the Republic

Women in Politics


Women's participation in politics contributes to the development of political processes and value systems. Increased representation of women in government can help diversify leadership approaches and promote greater accountability. Women in politics also play a key role in addressing national issues, advocating for policies on crime reduction, environmental conservation, education, and social welfare, ensuring that government resources are allocated to address diverse societal needs.

The trend of increasing female participation in politics is evident in the Philippines' public sector. Over the years, the number of elected women has consistently grown, except for a slight 2.6% decline in 2004, as reported by the Commission on Elections (Comelec). By 2016, women held 21.5% of all elected positions‚Äîthe highest percentage recorded in the past seven election cycles.

Additionally, the 2016 elections saw the highest proportion of female candidates at 19.4%, demonstrating a growing willingness among women to run for public office. This figure includes candidates across all elective roles in the executive and legislative branches, as well as those in local government units.





Despite this progress, women still make up a minority in the political landscape.

Women remain a minority in politics primarily because fewer women than men run for public office.

Despite gaining the right to vote in 1937 and increasing their participation as voters, Filipino women continue to be underrepresented in elective positions.

Reasons:

1.Lack of Encouragement and Support

Unlike men, women are often discouraged from pursuing political careers, making it harder for them to enter and succeed in the political arena. Women who aspire to enter politics often do not receive the same level of encouragement as men from their families, political parties, and society. Politics has long been a male-dominated field, and women frequently face skepticism about their ability to lead, making it harder for them to secure nominations or financial backing for campaigns.F

2. Gender Bias and Stereotypes

Women in politics face scrutiny not just for their policies but also for their appearance, personal lives, and behavior in ways that men do not. They are often subjected to sexist attacks, making it even more difficult for them to establish credibility and maintain public support.

The perception that leadership is a MAN'S ROLE continues to limit women‚Äôs opportunities in governance.

3. Cultural Expectations and Gender Roles

Traditional gender roles place a heavier burden of domestic responsibilities on women, making it challenging for them to balance political careers with societal expectations of being primary caregivers. Many women are discouraged from pursuing leadership positions because their political ambitions are seen as secondary to their family duties. While women's growing presence in politics is expected to bring positive changes‚Äîsuch as reducing corruption and prioritizing social issues‚Äîtheir low representation highlights the ongoing challenges they face in achieving equal political participation.

Having more women in governance leads to practical benefits, not just idealistic hopes:


1. More Diverse Perspectives in Decision-Making



Politics has long been male-dominated, meaning policies and laws are often shaped by male experiences and priorities. When women are in leadership roles, they bring different perspectives, particularly on healthcare, education, labor rights, and family welfare‚Äîissues that directly affect households and communities.
For example, in the Philippines, female lawmakers have been at the forefront of legislation on domestic violence (RA 9262), reproductive health (RH Law), and maternal care. These are issues that might not have received the same attention if women were not in positions of power.


2. A More Inclusive and Representative Government



Women make up about half of the population, yet they remain underrepresented in political offices. Reality Check



Since independence, the Philippines has always had at least one female legislator, and women have slowly gained more positions in local and national government.

However, political dynasties still dominate, meaning that many women in politics come from powerful families rather than grassroots movements.

Women who enter politics still face gendered attacks, where they are criticized more for their appearance, personal lives, or emotional reactions than their actual policies.
